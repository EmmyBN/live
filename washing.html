Washing Methods


A typical modern gas turbine, in simple cycle, converts only around 40% of the energy it consumes into useful MW output. Most of the power is actually used to drive the compressor, it will ideally be operating as efficiently as possible, and this means being as clean as possible.


The problem is that in spite of all the latest advances in filter technology, millions of undesirable microscopic particles will still be drawn into the turbine every second. Most of these particles will pass harmlessly through the compressor, but some will stick to the blades and to each other. Over time deposits (fouling) will build-up, causing an undesirable roughness. In a gas turbine compressor this means a deterioration in the aerodynamic form of the aerofoil blades, resulting in reduced air flow, higher air temperature and a lower pressure ratio which mean reduced efficiency and output, and increased environmental damage. There is also the issue of corrosive depositions which can lead to irreversible damage.


This dirt (or fouling as it is more commonly known) is the single biggest cause of performance losses, accounting for 85% of total of the total gas turbine losses. It is therefore a very serious and potentially costly problem, so compressors must be cleaned regularly to optimise their performance.


Washing is the best way to do this, although fouling tends to be insoluble and resistant to cleaning with water alone, and where the temperatures are high enough, can be baked-on to surfaces, sometimes forming fairly thick coatings which are difficult to remove. Using a powerful detergent like ProtoKlenz GT will therefore ensure all dirt is removed quickly, safely and completely.



Off-Line (Soak or Crank) Washing

Off-line washing is conducted with the gas turbine in a cooled state, by injecting a cleaning solution into the compressor while it is being turned at cranking speed


Once the chemicals are injected into the compressor, the gas turbine is shut off and allowed to come to a halt. The chemicals are then left to soak for 20-30 minutes, before being rinsed with demineralised or deionised water.


The main drawback is the amount of time the turbine must be taken out of operation to allow it to cool and be prepared for washing. The efficiency of crank washes however is very high, and the power recovery is close to the original level or the level reached after a major overhaul.


On-Line (Fired) Washing

This involves regularly spraying a cleaning solution into a compressor running at full speed. High operating temperatures inside the compressor, the high centrifugal forces on the injected liquid and the short contact time of the wash solution with the fouling all limit the effectiveness of this method.


The washing fluid will however reach the inlet guide vanes and first stage compressor blades which will result in some power recovery. This improves availability by slowing the rate of loss of output and prolonging the time between off-line washes. Using a suitable detergent will improve the wetting abilities of the wash solution, improving contact with the fouling so improving the cleaning effect, and also reducing the total amount of liquid required per wash.
